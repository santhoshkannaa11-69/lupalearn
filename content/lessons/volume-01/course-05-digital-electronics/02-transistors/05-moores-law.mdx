## 1. Hook

In 1965, Gordon Moore predicted that the number of transistors on a chip would double every two years. For 50 years, this prediction held true — driving the exponential growth that made smartphones, AI, and the modern internet possible.

## 2. Moore's Law in Practice

```
1971: 2,300 transistors (Intel 4004)
1980: 29,000 (8086)
1990: 1.2 million (80486)
2000: 42 million (Pentium 4)
2010: 2.3 billion (Core i7)
2024: 184 billion (M3 Ultra)
```

## 3. Why It's Slowing

| Challenge | Why It's Hard |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Atomic limits** | Transistors are now only a few atoms wide |
| **Heat** | More transistors = more heat |
| **Quantum effects** | Electrons leak through atom-scale barriers |
| **Cost** | A modern fab costs $20+ billion |

## 4. Summary

- **Moore's Law**: transistor count doubles every ~2 years
- Drove exponential computing growth for 50 years
- Now slowing due to **physical limits**
- Future gains will come from **architecture, not just scaling**
