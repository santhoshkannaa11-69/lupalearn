## 1. Hook

Your phone's weather app shows 72°F and a sunny icon. That's useful — you know to dress lightly.

But somewhere, a meteorologist is looking at thousands of raw temperature readings, pressure maps, and satellite images — making predictions that affect millions of people.

Why does the same "data" mean something different to each of them?

## 2. Why This Matters

The difference between data and information is one of the most important concepts in computing. Every program you write transforms raw data into useful information. Understanding this transformation is the difference between code that merely runs and code that actually *helps* people.

## 3. Learning Objectives

- **Differentiate** data, information, knowledge, and wisdom
- **Explain** the DIKW pyramid
- **Trace** how raw data becomes actionable wisdom

## 4. Core Concept

### The DIKW Pyramid

```
        ┌─────────┐
        │ Wisdom  │  ← Applied knowledge
        ├─────────┤
        │Knowledge│  ← Connected information
        ├─────────┤
        │Information│ ← Processed data
        ├─────────┤
        │  Data   │  ← Raw facts
        └─────────┘
```

### Example: A Hospital

| Level | Example | What It Is |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Data** | `98.6, 120/80, 37` | Raw numbers |
| **Information** | `Temperature: 98.6°F, BP: 120/80, Pulse: 37` | Labeled data |
| **Knowledge** | `Vitals are normal for a resting adult` | Patterns in data |
| **Wisdom** | `Patient is healthy — discharge with standard advice` | Decision based on knowledge |

## 5. Mental Model — The Kitchen Analogy

```
Data           → Raw ingredients (flour, eggs, sugar)
Information    → Measured ingredients (2 cups flour, 3 eggs, 1 cup sugar)
Knowledge      → A recipe combining them correctly
Wisdom         → Knowing when to adjust the recipe
```

You can give someone the same ingredients. What they do with them depends on their level of understanding.

## 6. Real-World Applications

| System | Data | Information | Action |
|--------|------|-------------|--------|
| **Navigation** | Satellite signals | "Turn left in 200m" | Route recalculation |
| **E-commerce** | Click history | "Customers also bought..." | Product recommendation |
| **Healthcare** | Symptom inputs | "85% match with condition X" | Suggested diagnosis |
| **Banking** | Transaction amounts | "Unusual spending pattern" | Fraud alert |

## 9. Summary

- **Data**: Raw facts (no context)
- **Information**: Data with meaning
- **Knowledge**: Patterns in information
- **Wisdom**: Applied knowledge for decisions
- Every program you write transforms data into information
