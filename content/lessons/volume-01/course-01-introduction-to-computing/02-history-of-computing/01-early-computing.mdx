## 1. Hook

Before electricity, before transistors, before silicon — humans were still computing. They used devices made of wood, bone, and metal to calculate everything from taxes to celestial navigation.

## 2. Why This Matters

The problems computers solve haven't changed — only the speed. Early computing devices reveal the fundamental challenge: how to automate repetitive calculation.

## 3. Early Devices Timeline

| Device | Year | Innovation |
|--------|------|------------|
| **Abacus** | ~2500 BCE | First positional number system |
| **Napier's Bones** | 1617 | Multiplication reduced to addition |
| **Slide Rule** | 1620 | Analog logarithmic computation |
| **Pascaline** | 1642 | First mechanical calculator |
| **Difference Engine** | 1822 | First automatic computation |

## 4. The Abacus — Mental Model

Think of the abacus as a manual memory system. Each column is a digit place (ones, tens, hundreds). Beads above the bar represent 5, beads below represent 1.

```
  ↓↓↓  ← heaven (each = 5)
━━━━━━━━━━━
  ↑↑↑  ← earth (each = 1)

Columns:  Thousands | Hundreds | Tens | Ones
```

Skilled abacus users can perform calculations faster than electronic calculators for addition and subtraction.

## 5. Why These Devices Matter

Every device on this timeline introduced an idea still used in modern computers:

| Device | Idea That Survives |
|--------|-------------------|
| Abacus | Positional representation |
| Napier's Bones | Reducing complex operations to simpler ones |
| Pascaline | Mechanical computation with gears |
| Difference Engine | Programmable automatic computation |

## 6. Summary

- Humans built computing devices for **thousands of years before electricity**
- Early devices introduced ideas that still underpin modern computing
- The problems (calculation, automation, accuracy) haven't changed — only the speed
