## 1. Hook

In the 1840s — decades before the first electric computer — a woman wrote a program for a machine that didn't exist yet. Her notes described concepts that wouldn't be realized for another 100 years.

## 2. Why This Matters

Babbage and Lovelace separated the *idea* of a computer from its physical implementation. They imagined a general-purpose machine before the technology to build it existed.

## 3. Babbage's Analytical Engine (1837)

| Component | What It Did | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **The Mill** | Performed arithmetic | CPU |
| **The Store** | Held numbers in memory | RAM |
| **Punch Cards** | Provided instructions | Program/Code |
| **Conditional branching** | Made decisions | If/else statements |
| **Loops** | Repeated operations | For/while loops |

## 4. Ada Lovelace — The First Programmer

Ada Lovelace wrote an algorithm to compute Bernoulli numbers on the Analytical Engine. Her contribution wasn't just the algorithm — it was the *insight* that the machine could process anything representable by symbols, not just numbers.

> "The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns, just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves."
> — Ada Lovelace

## 5. Why This Still Matters Today

Lovelace understood that computers are **general-purpose symbol processors**. This is why the same device can edit a document, play music, browse the web, and simulate a galaxy — because all of these are just different patterns of symbols.

## 6. Summary

- Babbage designed the first **general-purpose computer** (Analytical Engine)
- Lovelace wrote the **first algorithm** intended for machine execution
- She understood computers could process **any symbolic information**, not just numbers
- Their ideas were theoretical — the technology to build them came a century later

## 7. What's Next

A century after Babbage, **Alan Turing** would formalize the theory of computation and help build the first electronic computers.
