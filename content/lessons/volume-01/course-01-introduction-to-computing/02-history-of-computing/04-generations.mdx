## Generations of Computers

### First Generation (1940-1956): Vacuum Tubes

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Technology** | Vacuum tubes for circuits |
| **Size** | Room-sized (ENIAC: 1,800 sq ft) |
| **Speed** | ~5,000 operations/second |
| **Examples** | ENIAC, UNIVAC I |
| **Downside** | Generated immense heat, frequently failed |

### Second Generation (1956-1963): Transistors

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Technology** | Transistors replaced vacuum tubes |
| **Size** | Smaller, more reliable |
| **Speed** | ~100,000 operations/second |
| **Language** | Assembly language, early COBOL/Fortran |

### Third Generation (1964-1971): Integrated Circuits

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Technology** | Multiple transistors on one chip (IC) |
| **Size** | Minicomputers emerged |
| **Examples** | IBM System/360 |

### Fourth Generation (1971-Present): Microprocessors

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Technology** | Entire CPU on one chip |
| **Size** | Personal computers, laptops, phones |
| **Examples** | Intel 4004 → modern CPUs |

### Fifth Generation (Present-Future): AI & Parallel Processing

- AI and machine learning
- Quantum computing
- Neuromorphic chips
- Natural language interfaces

Each generation brought exponential increases in power and decreases in size.
