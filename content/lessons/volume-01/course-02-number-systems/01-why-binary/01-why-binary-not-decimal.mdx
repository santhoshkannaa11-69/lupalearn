## 1. Hook

Humans count in 10s (0-9). Computers count in 2s (0-1). Why? The answer is simpler than you'd think: because computers are made of switches.

## 2. Why This Matters

Understanding why computers use binary is the key to understanding how computers work at the most fundamental level — from memory to processors to networks.

## 3. Mental Model — The Light Switch

Think of a binary digit (bit) as a **light switch**:

```
Switch OFF = 0
Switch ON  = 1
```

A light switch has exactly two states — on or off. This is binary. A decimal digit would require a switch with 10 positions — much harder to build reliably at microscopic scales.

## 4. Core Concept

### The Switch Metaphor

Computers use **transistors** — tiny electronic switches. A transistor is either open (0, no current) or closed (1, current flowing). Billions of these switches on a chip work together to represent all data.

### Why Not Decimal?

| Aspect | Binary (base-2) | Decimal (base-10) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **States per digit** | 2 | 10 |
| **Circuit complexity** | Simple | Complex |
| **Reliability** | High (noise tolerant) | Low (noise sensitive) |
| **Physical size** | Small (less circuitry) | Large (10-state circuits) |

## 5. Historical Context

The first electronic computer (ENIAC, 1945) actually used decimal — each digit was represented by a ring of 10 vacuum tubes. It was enormous, power-hungry, and unreliable. Later computers adopted binary because transistors could only reliably represent two states.

## 7. Summary

- Computers use **binary** (base-2) because they're built from **switches**
- Each switch has **two states**: on (1) and off (0)
- Binary is simpler, more reliable, and more efficient than decimal for electronics
