## 1. Hook

In 1854, an English mathematician published a book called *The Laws of Thought*. In it, he described a system where logical statements could be expressed as mathematical equations. A century later, engineers realized his "algebra of logic" was exactly what they needed to design digital circuits.

## 2. Who Was George Boole?

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Name** | George Boole (1815-1864) |
| **Occupation** | Mathematician, logician |
| **Key work** | *The Laws of Thought* (1854) |
| **Contribution** | Created Boolean algebra — logic expressed as math |
| **Legacy** | Every programming language, every CPU, every digital circuit uses his ideas |

## 3. Boolean Algebra vs Regular Algebra

| Regular Algebra | Boolean Algebra |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Variables hold numbers | Variables hold true/false |
| Operations: +, -, ×, ÷ | Operations: AND, OR, NOT |
| Values range infinitely | Values are only 0 and 1 |
| Used for calculation | Used for decision-making |

## 4. Why It Changed Computing

Before Boolean algebra, logic was philosophy. After Boolean algebra, logic became **engineering**. Claude Shannon's 1937 master's thesis showed that Boolean algebra could be used to design telephone switching circuits — and that was the birth of digital logic design.

## 5. Summary

- **George Boole** created a mathematical system for logic
- **Boolean algebra** treats true/false as values and logical operations as math
- This became the foundation for **digital circuit design** and all modern computing
